02 · The challenge

Build an AI agent that drives a real robot — using vision and voice — in two hours.

We don't know which robot. We don't know the task. We find out at 10:30. That's the whole game.

Imagine giving a Roomba real ears (it understands what you say), real eyes (it understands what's in front of it), and a small brain that decides what to do next. That's the agent. Same idea as a self-driving car, but in 2 hours and on a different robot every time.

Because the robot is a surprise, we've spent weeks building a base that can plug into any robot in 30 minutes. Tomorrow we don't write a robot agent from scratch — we connect the last cable.

03 · How it actually works

Voice + camera → small brain → action — five times per second.

The same four-step loop runs every tick, in this order:

🎤
Step 1 · Ears
You say something out loud.
A small program writes your words down ("turn left and pick up the cup").
📷
Step 2 · Eyes
The camera takes a picture, twice a second.
Another program looks at it: "I see a red cup, a chair, and a person."
🧠
Step 3 · Brain
The brain reads both notes and decides one move.
"Turn 30° left, then walk forward 0.5 m." Picks the next thing to say back.
🦾
Step 4 · Body
The robot executes the move and speaks the line.
Loop back to step 1. Never stops until we say "done".
The whole loop happens in under 2 seconds, otherwise it feels laggy. That's why we run the brain on the powerful machines NVIDIA gives us, not on a laptop. Every box you'll hear us say tomorrow — vLLM, Nemotron, Ollama — is a different way of running that brain.
04 · Inside the brain

How the robot decides — four tricks, fastest first.

All scenarios share the same brain. Only the voice cue, obstacles, and goal change. The brain picks one of four strategies per command.

⚡️ 1. Memorized recipes
For common cues like "spin 360", "go to the bin", or "10 steps forward", the brain has pre-written kinematic sequences. No thinking, no LLM call — instant. Like muscle memory.
🧩 2. Decompose & plan
For new cues like "pick up the red cube and put it in the bin", the brain breaks it into steps: 1. Move to cube. 2. Grasp. 3. Move to bin. 4. Release. Then executes one step per tick.
🗺️ 3. A* path-finding
When obstacles block the path, the brain draws a map, computes the shortest collision-free route as waypoints, and walks them. Like Google Maps for a robot floor.
↩️ 4. Reactive dodge
If something appears suddenly mid-step, the brain sidesteps and continues. The "oh!" reflex — between full plans, no re-thinking required.
If the robot stalls for ~6 seconds, it re-injects the cue for a fresh plan. If still stuck, it stops and waits for new instructions. Never invents filler behaviour — that's our hard rule.

The weird words you'll hear tomorrow.

VLM"vee-el-em" · Vision-Language Model
An LLM that also sees pictures. We give it the camera frame and ask: "describe what's in front of you."Same assistant, but now also wearing glasses.
Nemotron 3 Nano Omni"NEH-mo-tron Omni"
The brain NVIDIA pre-loaded on the hackathon machines. Multimodal — does both LLM and VLM jobs at once. One program sees and thinks.A two-in-one assistant. Less wiring for us.
Qwen 3.6 35B A3B"kwen thirty-five bee"
The backup brain. Made by Alibaba. Only does text — no eyes. Fallback if Nemotron's vision is broken.A blind genius. Smart, but needs us to describe the room.
vLLM"vee-el-el-em"
The engine that runs the brain on the hackathon computer. The gasoline that makes Nemotron go fast. Pre-installed.The kitchen the chef (the brain) needs to actually cook.
Ollama"oh-LAH-ma"

ZGX Nano"zee-jee-ex nano"
The two AI computers HP gives us at the venue. Brains run there; we control them remotely from the laptop. The prize is one each per teammate.A tiny supercomputer the size of a Mac mini.

06 · Your roles

Without your laptops, you are the difference between winning and losing.

The build runs on Timur's laptop only — the robot only listens to one machine anyway. Your jobs are higher-leverage.

⌨️ Listener-typist
During the 30-min intro, type what the speaker says into DAY_OF_BRIEF.md on Timur's laptop. Don't worry about structure — write whatever you hear. Claude turns it into config edits in 5 min.
🎬 Demo lead
From T+1:30 onward: record the demo on your phone (backup video), check the dashboard renders, narrate the 60-second judged run. Dashboard is our biggest visible feature — keep it on screen.
⏱️ Time keeper
We live and die by the clock. Call out loudly: "T+1:00", "T+1:30", "T+1:45 — freeze!". If the room is loud, walk over and tap shoulders. Non-negotiable.
✂️ Cut-list enforcer
At T+1:30 if we're behind, someone must say "we drop X". Timur is too deep in the code to see it. Cuts are pre-decided (next section). You just call them.
👁️ Quality eyes
Watch the robot move. If it's about to crash, hit the kill switch. If it speaks gibberish or freezes, tell us. Our heads are in code — yours are on the floor.
☕ Outside ops
Coffee runs, asking organizers questions, making sure we eat at 12:30. Sounds small — until your blood sugar tanks at T+1:45.

08 · Plan B (and C, D, E)

The cut-list — we drop things on purpose, in this exact order.

Cutting is not failure. Cutting is the plan. The team that ships a smaller working demo beats the team that ships nothing.

T+1:00
Cut #1. If the microphone keeps missing your voice or speech-to-text is wrong half the time…
…we drop the microphone. Type cues into the dashboard instead. Same robot, just no voice in.
T+1:15
Cut #2. If the robot's voice (TTS) is laggy or makes the loop slow…
…we drop the robot's voice. Show subtitles instead. Robot still does everything else.
T+1:30
Cut #3. If juggling two AI computers gets brittle (network drops, sync issues)…
…we drop the second computer. Run on one. Slower, but rock-solid.
T+1:45
Cut #4 (last resort). If the robot is crashing or the adapter is flaky…
…we show a video of an earlier run + drive a fake robot live. Judges see the agent thinking; the physical robot stays parked.
Never cut: the dashboard, the JSONL logging, the adapter contract.
